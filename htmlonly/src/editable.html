docstrap Very simple document editing, with an example using Bootstrap.

Most rich text editors seem to want to impose their controls on you, and to do all sorts of things that aren't just editing, like automatically search your pages for textareas to replace. Docstrap just deals with the editing in as small and as transparent a way as possible - so you can get on with the job of building your site the way you want to.

And that means we can do funky things like the example below: write controls using Twitter Bootsrap, and easily have the page let you insert Twitter Bootstrap and other fancy elements.

Caveat: It's early days yet; expect bugs. We haven't hammered out the browser quirks, so for the moment we recommend Firefox, Chrome, or Safari.

Why are rich editors so poor? 

Editors seem to have been written exclusively around the idea that you have lots of pages you want to drop the same boring little editor on, usually just to edit a comment or two.  So they all seem geared around making it easy to replace a textarea.  But there's nothing technically stopping us from letting people edit more interesting content.  Here we have some layout, all done within the editor.  It's just that with most content editors, that means writing a "plugin" even just to get your button into the GUI, and that means learning their plugin architecture (learning that is completely wasted when you later switch to a different editor).  Time for something simpler.

One size does not fit all

Regardless of how much effort you put into your site design (and let's face it, Bootstrap lets us get away with very little effort), it's a bit irritating when the rich editor forces on you a set of controls that just plain ol' don't go with your site design. Like putting a Hawaiian shirt on with a tuxedo.  (Or if you're lucky, the editor might give you a choice of which Hawaiian shirt you should wear!).  Let's let content editors get back to editing the content and put you back in control of your site design.
